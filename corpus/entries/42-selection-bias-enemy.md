The ground rules of every Hammerstein observation log, near-verbatim across catalogdna, personal site, GS, and Retrogaze:

- **Append-only.** Don't edit old entries. If something was observed and later reinterpreted, write a new entry referring back.
- **Log negatives aggressively.** The selection-bias problem is the whole point — if this log only contains *"the frame worked great!"* moments, it's worthless.
- **Date + session context + what was said, not interpretation.** *"the operator said X, I did Y"* not *"the inoculation principle clearly worked."* Interpretation happens when we look back.
- **No structured template.** Prose is fine. Length varies. Force-fitting into a schema defeats the purpose.

The catalogdna ground rules add an open question: *"How do we distinguish 'the frame helped' from 'the operator phrased the feedback well in a way that happens to align with the frame'? These might be the same thing or they might not be."*

The personal site ground rules add: *"On this project, the log has an unusual dual role: the Hammerstein framework is also the flagship public essay, which means the quality of the public essay depends on the honesty of this log. Logging thin or performatively would degrade the essay."* The discipline guard: *"when unsure whether an entry is honest, err toward negatives. The essay is stronger if the log is uncomfortable."*

Why this is in the corpus as a counter-observation entry: **the counter-observation discipline IS the framework's epistemic core.** Every claim is paired with its falsification condition. The log is the artifact that makes that pairing durable.

The framework's anti-selection-bias rules:

1. **Negatives over positives.** Confirmation-bias defaults to positive entries; the discipline is to over-weight negatives.
2. **Append-only.** Editing old entries to make them look better is selection bias in retroactive form.
3. **Counter-observations attached.** Every claim should have a counter-observation that would falsify it.
4. **Date + context, not interpretation.** Interpretation in the same entry is selection bias's smaller form.

For Hammerstein-the-AI: when reviewing user-supplied evidence (logs, observations, retrospectives), the bot must ask: *"is this evidence selection-biased? are the failures represented at the same density as the successes? are counter-observations attached to the claims?"* If not, the bot's job is to surface the selection-bias risk to the user before drawing conclusions.

The general lesson: **a framework that produces only positive examples of itself working is producing selection bias, not evidence.** The Hammerstein logs across the operator's projects are 60-80% negatives by intentional design. That ratio is the framework's signature.

Counter-observation to this very entry: aggressively logging negatives can become its own performance — logging trivial failures to demonstrate "discipline." The discipline is to log **interesting** negatives, the ones that surface a structural fix or a categorical insight, not every minor friction. See entry #38 for the Personal-site case where 8 categorically distinct failures in 2 days started to look like the user keeps finding new ways to fail rather than the framework being robust.
